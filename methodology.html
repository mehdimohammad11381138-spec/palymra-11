Methodology

Research Approach

This project follows a systematic methodology combining Knowledge Graph exploration with Large Language Model enrichment. Our approach identifies gaps in existing cultural heritage data and proposes structured enhancements through RDF triples and vocabulary extensions.

The methodology is grounded in the KE4H (Knowledge Engineering for Heritage) framework, which emphasizes the integration of structured and unstructured knowledge sources to create more comprehensive and accurate representations of cultural heritage.

Step 1: Topic and Knowledge Graph Selection

Topic Selected

"The Authenticity of Tourism in Palmyra: A Case Study of Composite Authenticity in Post-Conflict Heritage Sites"

Knowledge Graphs Used

  • Wikidata: Comprehensive linked open data with properties for heritage designation, restoration status, and cultural significance
  • DBpedia: Structured extraction of Wikipedia data with detailed descriptions of archaeological sites

Rationale

Palmyra is a globally significant UNESCO World Heritage Site with extensive documentation in both KGs. These sources allow us to extract existing data and identify what is missing regarding post-conflict restoration and authenticity assessment.

Step 2: Knowledge Graph Vocabulary Exploration

Before writing queries, we explored the ontologies and vocabularies used in Wikidata and DBpedia to understand how cultural properties are described.

Key Properties Identified in Wikidata

  • P1435 — Heritage designation (e.g., World Heritage Site)
  • P31 — Instance of (e.g., archaeological site, temple)
  • P131 — Located in (geographic location)
  • P580 — Start time (construction or founding date)
  • P582 — End time (destruction or abandonment date)

Key Classes in DBpedia

  • dbo:WorldHeritageSite — UNESCO World Heritage designation
  • dbo:ArchaeologicalSite — Archaeological significance
  • dbo:Building — Architectural structure

Step 3: Gap Identification

Through systematic exploration of the knowledge graphs, we identified critical gaps in the representation of Palmyra's heritage data:

Gap 1: Missing Authenticity Metadata

Current KGs describe Palmyra as a "World Heritage Site in Danger" but lack structured data about the different dimensions of authenticity (material, experiential, interpretive) and how they have been affected by conflict and restoration.

Gap 2: Incomplete Restoration Information

While some monuments (e.g., Lion of Al-Lat) have restoration records, there is no systematic documentation of restoration methods (Anastylosis, reconstruction, stabilization) or the actors involved (DGAM, UNESCO, Hermitage, ALIPH).

Gap 3: Lack of Temporal Authenticity Tracking

KGs do not capture how authenticity has evolved over time: pre-conflict (fragile but materially sound), during conflict (destroyed), and post-restoration (composite authenticity).

Gap 4: Missing Community and Stakeholder Data

There is no structured representation of the role of local communities, international organizations, and restoration experts in the heritage preservation process.

Step 4: Research Questions Formulated

Based on the identified gaps, we formulated specific research questions to guide our enrichment work:

  1. RQ1: How can we systematically represent the concept of "Composite Authenticity" in a knowledge graph, incorporating material, experiential, and interpretive dimensions?
  2. RQ2: What structured data about restoration methods and actors can be extracted from academic sources and LLMs to enrich the KG?
  3. RQ3: How can we model the temporal evolution of authenticity status (pre-conflict, during conflict, post-restoration) in RDF?
  4. RQ4: What differences exist between LLM-generated knowledge and authoritative academic sources regarding heritage restoration?
